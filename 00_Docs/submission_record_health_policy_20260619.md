## 4. Abstract（投稿版・250語以内）

> **Background:** Universal health coverage should align evidence-based care with clinical need, yet regional delivery may remain supply-sensitive. Perioperative oral functional management (POFM) is nationally reimbursed in Japan since 2012, but whether provision reflects surgical demand or healthcare supply capacity is unknown.
>
> **Objectives:** To characterize prefectural variation in POFM delivery across Japan, identify its association with cancer surgical demand and dental workforce supply, and assess spatial clustering.
>
> **Methods:** Prefectural ecological study (N = 47) using Japan's 10th NDB Open Data (fiscal year 2023). The primary outcome was POFM claim density per 100,000 population; the need-side exposure was major cancer surgery density (16 K-codes). We applied OLS regression with HC3 robust standard errors, seven pre-specified sensitivity analyses, Global Moran's I, Lagrange Multiplier tests, and spatial lag regression.
>
> **Results:** POFM density varied 2.25-fold across 47 prefectures (68,804–154,850 per 100,000). Cancer surgery density was not significantly associated with POFM density in any sensitivity specification (0/7 significant). Dentist density was the strongest predictor (r = 0.617, p < 0.0001). Significant positive spatial autocorrelation was identified (Moran's I = 0.210, p = 0.024). The Spatial Lag Model confirmed a significant autoregressive coefficient (ρ = 0.354, p = 0.002), with cancer surgery density remaining non-significant.
>
> **Conclusions:** POFM delivery is supply-sensitive rather than need-aligned under Japan's universal coverage system. Equitable implementation may require simultaneous investment in dental workforce capacity, hospital–dental integration, and knowledge transfer alongside reimbursement.
