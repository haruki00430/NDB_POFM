## Scope and Relevance to Health Policy

This manuscript directly addresses a core tension in health policy: *does the introduction of universal insurance coverage for an evidence-based procedure translate into equitable, need-aligned implementation?* Using Japan's 47 prefectures as ecological units and the National Database (NDB) of Health Insurance Claims and Specific Health Checkups as the data source, we examine whether perioperative oral functional management (POFM) — a nationally reimbursed procedure since 2012 — is delivered according to clinical need or whether implementation is determined by healthcare supply capacity.

Our principal findings are:

- **POFM delivery varies 2.25-fold across 47 Japanese prefectures** (range 68,804–154,850 per 100,000 population), indicating substantial geographic inequity under a system of universal coverage.
- **Cancer surgical demand — the primary clinical need indicator — was not significantly associated with POFM provision in any of seven pre-specified sensitivity specifications** (0/7 at p < 0.05). This robust null finding persists across heteroscedasticity-consistent standard errors, outlier exclusion, metropolitan exclusion, log-transformation, and spatial lag modelling.
- **Dentist density was the strongest predictor of POFM delivery** (Pearson r = 0.617, p < 0.0001), and **significant positive spatial autocorrelation was identified** (Moran's I = 0.210, p = 0.024), suggesting that geographic clustering reflects supply infrastructure rather than clinical demand patterns.
- **Implications extend beyond Japan**: as reimbursement for perioperative oral care expands internationally, policymakers should recognise that financing alone may not guarantee equitable implementation. Workforce capacity, hospital–dental integration, referral pathways, and multidisciplinary collaboration appear to require simultaneous investment.

## Why Health Policy

These findings are directly relevant to *Health Policy*'s readership of researchers, policymakers, and managers concerned with health system performance, equity, and the translation of insurance coverage into access:

1. **Coverage–access gap**: This study quantifies the gap between formal entitlement (universal reimbursement) and realised access (prefecture-level POFM density) — a fundamental question in health policy research.
2. **Supply-side determinism of implementation**: Our findings add to the international literature showing that reimbursement without parallel supply-side investment may exacerbate geographic inequity, not reduce it.
3. **Policy design implications**: The spatial clustering of POFM delivery points to the need for region-specific implementation support — a concrete and actionable policy recommendation.
4. **International transferability**: Countries now incorporating perioperative oral care into their insurance systems (including several European nations and South Korea) face the same implementation challenge; Japan's experience provides an empirical case study.

## Study Strengths

- **Nationwide census**: All 47 Japanese prefectures included; no sampling bias.
- **Robust null finding**: Consistent across 7 pre-specified sensitivity specifications.
- **Spatial epidemiology**: Formal spatial autocorrelation analysis (Moran's I, LISA, spatial lag model) strengthens causal interpretation.
- **Transparent methods**: Analysis code will be made openly available via GitHub and Zenodo upon acceptance.
